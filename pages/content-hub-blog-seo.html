Using Content Hub's SEO Tools Without Losing Your Editorial Voice

SEO recommendations are a checklist, not a writing style. Here's where to follow them and where to ignore them.

AN

Adem Nadir

Published May 15, 2026 · Updated June 18, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Treat keyword and topic recommendations as research input, not sentence-level instructions
  • Structural recommendations (headings, internal links) are usually worth following; phrasing suggestions usually aren't
  • Content written to satisfy a score, not a reader, tends to underperform on engagement even when it ranks
  • Use the SEO tool's topic cluster suggestions to plan content, then write each piece independently

Content Hub's SEO recommendations engine is genuinely useful for research — it surfaces related keywords, content gaps, and topic clusters faster than manual research would. The mistake teams make is treating the on-page optimization score as the goal itself, rewriting sentences to hit a number rather than to communicate clearly. That produces content that scores well and reads like it was written for a machine, because it was.

What the recommendations are actually good for

Topic cluster mapping and keyword gap analysis are the strongest use of the tool — it can show you that competitors ranking for your target term also cover three related subtopics you haven't addressed, which is genuinely useful planning input. Internal linking suggestions are also reliable: the tool can identify existing posts on your site that should link to a new piece, which is tedious to track manually as a site grows.

Where to stop following the score

Sentence-level phrasing suggestions — inserting a keyword variant into a specific paragraph, hitting an exact keyword density target — are the part to treat as optional. Optimization scores reward pattern-matching, not clarity, and a sentence rewritten to satisfy the score often reads worse than the original. If a suggested edit makes a sentence sound less like something a person would actually say, that's a signal to skip it, not a gap to close.

Structure first, score second

The highest-leverage SEO work happens at the structural level before you write a word: a clear H1 with the primary keyword, a logical H2 hierarchy that mirrors how a reader would ask the question, and a meta description that states the actual value of the page rather than stuffing keywords. Get the structure right and the optimization score will mostly take care of itself without you needing to chase it sentence by sentence.

Plan with the tool, write without it

A workflow that holds up well in practice: use Content Hub's topic cluster and gap analysis to build your content calendar and outline each piece's subtopics, then close the tool and write the draft independently, focused on actually answering the reader's question well. Run the optimization check afterward as a final pass — fix anything structural it flags (missing alt text, no internal links, missing meta description), and ignore phrasing suggestions that would make the writing worse.
